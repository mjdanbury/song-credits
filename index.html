Where Credit Is Due

Album credit information on streaming platforms is often innacurate. Listeners are missing out.

By Matthew Danbury

Releasing a studio album is a labor of love. From songwriting and arranging, to recording, producing, mixing, and mastering there are many steps at which musical artists choose to bring other people onto a project to help out. This medley of tastes and process of bouncing ideas back and forth often leads to amazing work.

Back when albums were only released on vinyl records and CDs, a credit listing of everyone who worked on an album would be included in the album's liner notes. If you wanted to see who worked on a track you liked, you could be sure that you could find that information and that it would be accurate.

However, with the advent of streaming platforms, and self released music, this is no longer the case. Streaming platforms will often lack some or all of the credits for a given album, leaving listeners in the dark. Take for example, Frank Ocean's 2016 Album, Blonde:

Kanye West

André 3000

Beyoncé

Tyler, the Creator

Kanye West

André 3000

Beyoncé

Tyler, the Creator

Spotify lists seven people, including Frank Ocean, who worked on the album.

In fact, there are actually 82.

Including several A-list performers.

According to Michael Kushner, senior vice president at Warner Music Group, there are several points at which inaccuracies could enter the picture. "Record companies create label copy based on what we are told by the artist, who is responsible for delivering the recordings under our recording agreements. The A&R folks who work with the artists help insure accuracy, which is something we always strive for. That label copy is supplied as metadata to the streaming services, but the services themselves are not consistent about what data they display, and where."
